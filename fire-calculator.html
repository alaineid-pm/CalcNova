What Is FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early)?

FIRE is a movement built on aggressively saving and investing — typically 40–70% of income — to accumulate enough wealth that portfolio returns can fund your lifestyle indefinitely, freeing you from the need to work. The two pillars are the 25x rule (your FI number = annual expenses × 25) and the 4% safe withdrawal rate, which research shows can sustain a portfolio through most 30-year periods. FIRE isn't just for the ultra-wealthy — it's achievable by anyone who dramatically increases their savings rate, regardless of income.

How to Calculate Your FIRE Number

Your FIRE number = annual expenses × 25. If you spend $40,000/year, your FI number is $1,000,000. If you spend $60,000/year, it's $1,500,000. This is derived from the 4% rule: 4% of $1,000,000 = $40,000/year. The key insight is that reducing expenses does double duty — it both lowers your FIRE number and frees up more money to invest. Someone spending $30,000/year needs only $750,000 and can invest far more aggressively than someone spending $80,000/year chasing $2,000,000.

The 4% Rule — and Its Limits

The 4% rule comes from the 1998 Trinity Study, which found that a portfolio of 50–75% stocks and 25–50% bonds could sustain 4% annual withdrawals (inflation-adjusted) through any historical 30-year period. For early retirees with 40–50-year horizons, a more conservative 3–3.5% withdrawal rate reduces sequence-of-returns risk. Many FIRE practitioners build in flexibility: reduce spending in down markets, maintain some part-time income, or keep a cash buffer to avoid selling equities at a loss.

How Savings Rate Drives Your Timeline

Savings rate is the most powerful lever in your FIRE equation. At 10% savings rate, FI takes ~40 years. At 25%, ~32 years. At 50%, ~17 years. At 70%, ~8.5 years. This compresses dramatically because higher savings simultaneously grows your portfolio faster and lowers the target (lower expenses = smaller FIRE number). Increasing your savings rate by even 5–10 percentage points typically shaves 3–5 years off your timeline.

Types of FIRE

Lean FIRE: retiring on a minimal budget ($25,000–$40,000/year), requiring a smaller portfolio but less lifestyle flexibility. Fat FIRE: retiring with a generous budget ($100,000+/year) for travel, family, and comfort. Barista FIRE: semi-retirement with part-time work covering basic costs while investments compound. Coast FIRE: saving enough early so compound growth reaches your FI number by traditional retirement age — no more contributions needed. Use the Coast FIRE Calculator to find that milestone.

FIRE and Inflation

Inflation erodes purchasing power over time — $60,000 today will feel like significantly less in 20 years. This calculator lets you toggle real (inflation-adjusted) returns to see your timeline in today's dollars. Most practitioners use a real return of 5–7% (nominal ~8–10% minus ~3% inflation). Planning with real returns gives a more conservative and honest picture of when you can actually retire.
