Tipping Etiquette: When, Where, and How Much

Tipping has become more confusing than ever — tip screens on every counter, suggested percentages creeping upward, and different rules in different contexts. Here's a clear-headed guide to tipping in the modern era.

5 min readUpdated April 2026

A Brief History of Tipping

Tipping originated in 16th-century England, where the phrase "To Insure Promptness" was supposedly the source of the acronym TIP — though etymologists largely regard this as folk etymology. The practice arrived in the United States in the late 1800s, brought back by American travelers who had observed it in Europe. Early American labor advocates actually opposed tipping, viewing it as a vestige of servility incompatible with democratic values. A brief anti-tipping movement emerged in the early 1900s, but it was ultimately eclipsed by the restaurant industry's adoption of tipped wages as a legal substitute for minimum wage.

Today, tipped workers in the U.S. can legally be paid as little as $2.13 per hour at the federal level, with tips expected to make up the difference to at least minimum wage. This system — unique among developed nations — means that in the United States, tipping is not merely etiquette. For many service workers, it is the primary source of income. Understanding this context is essential for making thoughtful decisions about when and how much to tip.

Tipping at Restaurants: The 15/18/20 Debate

Sit-down restaurants remain the context where tipping norms are most established and most debated. The traditional standard of 15% has been steadily displaced by 18% and 20% as the new baseline in many cities, with suggested amounts on payment terminals often starting at 20% and going up to 25% or 30%.

The practical question: do you tip on the pre-tax or post-tax amount? Mathematically, the difference is small but real. On a $100 pre-tax bill with 10% tax, tipping 20% on the pre-tax total gives $20.00, while tipping on the post-tax total gives $22.00. Most etiquette guides recommend tipping on the pre-tax amount, though tipping on the full bill is also widely accepted and simpler to calculate.

Example: Restaurant Bill Calculation

You and a friend have dinner. The food and drinks total $85.00 before tax. Your city has 9% sales tax, bringing the bill to $92.65.

  • 15% tip on pre-tax: $85 × 0.15 = $12.75
  • 18% tip on pre-tax: $85 × 0.18 = $15.30
  • 20% tip on pre-tax: $85 × 0.20 = $17.00
  • 20% tip on post-tax: $92.65 × 0.20 = $18.53
A standard 18–20% tip on this meal: $15 – $17

A quick mental math shortcut: to calculate 20%, move the decimal point one place left (10% of $85 = $8.50), then double it ($17.00). For 15%, take 10% and add half ($8.50 + $4.25 = $12.75). These shortcuts work on any bill in seconds.

For exceptional service, 25% or more is appropriate. For genuinely poor service that was within the server's control, 10–12% signals dissatisfaction while still leaving something. Leaving nothing is generally reserved for truly egregious situations — and it's worth remembering that many service failures (slow kitchen, wrong order) are often outside the server's direct control.

Tipping Across Other Services

Beyond restaurants, tipping norms vary significantly by service type. The expansion of digital payment systems has introduced tip prompts in contexts where tipping was historically uncommon — coffee shops, fast-casual counters, and self-checkout kiosks — creating what some observers call "tip fatigue."

For food delivery, tip at the time of ordering rather than after — some platforms weight driver assignment toward orders with higher pre-set tips. Changing a tip downward after delivery is possible on some platforms but considered poor form unless there was a genuine problem.

Tipping Internationally: Read the Room

One of the most common travel mistakes Americans make is applying U.S. tipping norms abroad. In many countries, tipping customs differ dramatically — and in some, leaving a large tip can actually cause offense.

🌍 International Tipping Norms

Country-by-Country Quick Reference

  • United States & Canada: Tipping is expected and wages are structured around it. 18–20% at restaurants is standard.
  • United Kingdom: 10–15% at sit-down restaurants is appreciated; check if service charge is already included.
  • Western Europe (France, Germany, Spain, Italy): Rounding up or leaving 5–10% is polite; large tips are uncommon and sometimes viewed as condescending.
  • Japan: Tipping is considered rude in most contexts. Servers may return your tip. Service is regarded as a professional standard, not something earned through gratuity.
  • South Korea, China: Generally not expected; may cause confusion. Some upscale or tourist-facing restaurants accept tips.
  • Australia & New Zealand: Not expected but appreciated for exceptional service; 10% is generous.
  • Mexico, Latin America: Tipping is appreciated and somewhat expected at tourist-facing restaurants; 10–15% is typical.
  • Middle East (UAE, Egypt): Tipping is welcomed; 10–15% at restaurants, small cash tips for service staff.

Always check whether a service charge is already included in the bill — in many European countries, "service compris" or a line item for service means you're already paying a built-in tip.

When It's Acceptable Not to Tip

Despite the social pressure created by tip-screen prompts and default suggested amounts, there are genuine situations where tipping is not expected, not customary, or even inappropriate.

You are generally not expected to tip when:

  • Picking up your own takeout order at a counter (though a small tip is appreciated if the order was complex)
  • Using a self-serve kiosk or automated checkout
  • Visiting a business where the owner is serving you directly (tips are sometimes declined as awkward)
  • Paying a service fee that explicitly states it covers staff compensation
  • In countries where tipping is culturally unusual or actively discouraged

Tip fatigue is a real phenomenon — the proliferation of tip prompts at every transaction has left many people feeling pressured in contexts with no established norm. It's reasonable to decline tipping for counter service, self-checkout, or automated services without guilt. Reserve your tipping energy — and budget — for the service workers who depend on it most: restaurant servers, delivery drivers, hotel housekeepers, and personal care professionals.

If a service charge or gratuity is already included in your bill (common at large parties or upscale restaurants), you are not obligated to tip on top of it, though leaving a small additional amount for truly exceptional service is always appreciated.
