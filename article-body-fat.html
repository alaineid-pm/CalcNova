Body Fat Percentage: What the Numbers Mean

The scale tells you your weight. Body fat percentage tells you what that weight is made of. Understanding the difference can completely change how you approach fitness and health.

6 min readUpdated April 2026

What Body Fat Percentage Actually Measures

Body fat percentage is the proportion of your total body weight that is composed of fat tissue. The rest — your muscles, bones, organs, water, and connective tissue — is collectively called lean body mass (LBM). Body fat percentage is therefore a measure of your body composition, not just your size or weight.

Body weight is a blunt instrument. Two people can weigh exactly the same and have radically different health profiles — one might be muscular and lean, the other might carry significantly more fat and less muscle. Body fat percentage cuts through that ambiguity and provides a far more informative picture of your actual health and fitness status.

Body fat serves essential functions: it insulates organs, stores energy, produces hormones (including estrogen and leptin), and supports cell membrane integrity. The question isn't whether you need body fat — you do — but how much is optimal for your age, sex, and health goals.

Healthy Body Fat Ranges for Men and Women

Fat percentage norms differ significantly between men and women. Women require a higher minimum fat percentage due to sex-specific hormonal and reproductive fat stores. Men and women also tend to store fat in different locations: men more abdominally (visceral fat), women more in the hips, thighs, and subcutaneously.

The American Council on Exercise (ACE) categorizes body fat percentages into the following ranges:

Category Men Women Notes
Essential Fat 2–5% 10–13% Minimum needed for basic physiological function
Athletic 6–13% 14–20% Typical of competitive athletes and very active individuals
Fitness 14–17% 21–24% Above average fitness; healthy and sustainable
Average 18–24% 25–31% Acceptable but associated with some metabolic risk
Obese 25%+ 32%+ Elevated risk for cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and other conditions

Note that these are general population norms. Elite endurance athletes may fall into the "essential fat" range and still be perfectly healthy. Context — including training history, age, and overall health markers — always matters more than a single number.

Essential Fat vs. Storage Fat

Not all body fat is equal. Understanding the two main types helps clarify why some fat is necessary and unavoidable, while other fat accumulation is what we're working to manage.

Essential fat is the fat stored in organs, bone marrow, the central nervous system, and cell membranes. It is necessary for survival and normal physiological function. In women, additional essential fat is stored in the breasts, pelvis, and hips to support reproductive health. Falling below essential fat levels causes serious hormonal disruption, bone loss, and organ damage — this is what is seen in cases of extreme eating disorders.

Storage fat is the fat accumulated in adipose tissue beneath the skin (subcutaneous fat) and around internal organs (visceral fat). Subcutaneous fat serves as an energy reserve and insulates the body. Visceral fat — the fat packed around abdominal organs — is metabolically active and is more strongly linked to insulin resistance, inflammation, and cardiovascular disease than subcutaneous fat.

This distinction matters practically: someone with a seemingly healthy total body fat percentage can still carry dangerous amounts of visceral fat. Waist circumference (above 40 inches for men, 35 inches for women) is often used as a proxy for visceral fat risk when detailed measurement isn't available.

How Measurement Methods Compare

Body fat percentage can be measured through several methods, each with different accuracy levels, costs, and accessibility. Knowing their limitations helps you interpret your results correctly.

Accuracy vs. Accessibility

Method Accuracy Cost Best For

Hydrostatic Weighing ±1.5–2% $$ Research / clinical use
Skinfold Calipers ±3–5% $ Tracking trends with a skilled tester

For most people, the US Navy formula is the best starting point — it's free, requires only a tape measure, and has reasonable accuracy. DEXA scans are worth the investment if you want a precise baseline or are making clinical decisions.

BMI deserves special mention because it is so widely used despite being a poor proxy for body fat percentage. BMI divides weight by height squared and was designed as a population-level statistical tool — it cannot distinguish muscle from fat. A muscular athlete may show an "overweight" BMI while carrying 10% body fat. Conversely, "normal weight obesity" — a healthy BMI with high body fat and low muscle mass — is common and invisible to BMI measurement.

The US Navy Body Fat Formula

The US Navy method estimates body fat percentage from circumference measurements and height. It requires no special equipment beyond a flexible measuring tape — making it by far the most accessible reasonably accurate method available.

Men: BF% = 86.010 × log10(abdomen − neck) − 70.041 × log10(height) + 36.76

Women: BF% = 163.205 × log10(waist + hip − neck) − 97.684 × log10(height) − 78.387
abdomen / waist = circumference at navel level (inches) neck = circumference at narrowest point (inches) hip = widest circumference (women only, inches) height = in inches
Example: Navy Formula for a Man

Inputs: Height = 70 in, Neck = 15 in, Abdomen = 34 in

BF% = 86.010 × log10(34 − 15) − 70.041 × log10(70) + 36.76

= 86.010 × log10(19) − 70.041 × log10(70) + 36.76

= 86.010 × 1.2788 − 70.041 × 1.8451 + 36.76

= 109.99 − 129.22 + 36.76 = ~17.5% body fat  →  Fitness category

Measure consistently for best results: tape snug but not compressing skin, at the same time of day (ideally morning), and take three measurements and average them. The Navy formula's margin of error is 3–4%, so use it to track trends over time rather than fixate on a single reading.

How to Improve Your Body Composition

Body composition improvement — reducing fat mass while preserving or building lean mass — is a different goal than simply losing weight. A scale-focused approach can lead to losing muscle along with fat, which worsens your body fat percentage even as your weight drops. The goal is to change the ratio, not just the total.

The most effective strategies combine resistance training (to build and preserve muscle), a modest caloric deficit (to reduce fat without triggering muscle breakdown), and adequate protein intake (to support muscle protein synthesis during a deficit).

  • Resistance training: 2–4 sessions per week of compound movements (squats, deadlifts, rows, presses) is the strongest stimulus for muscle retention and growth during fat loss.
  • Protein intake: Research consistently supports 0.7–1.0 grams of protein per pound of bodyweight during fat loss phases to preserve lean mass.
  • Caloric deficit: A deficit of 300–500 calories per day is typically sustainable and minimizes muscle loss. Larger deficits accelerate fat loss but also accelerate lean mass loss and increase hormonal stress.
  • Sleep and recovery: Poor sleep elevates cortisol (which promotes fat storage and muscle breakdown) and suppresses growth hormone (which promotes fat burning and muscle repair). Sleep is a body composition tool.
  • Progressive overload: Consistently increasing training demands over time is necessary to continue building muscle once you've adapted to a given workload.

Most people can expect to lose 0.5–1% of body weight per week as fat while maintaining muscle on an optimized plan. Progress tracked via body fat percentage — rather than just the scale — gives a much more accurate and motivating picture of actual results.
