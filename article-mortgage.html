How to Choose the Right Mortgage

A mortgage is likely the largest financial commitment you'll ever make. Understanding the key variables — loan type, term, rate, and fees — can save you tens of thousands of dollars over the life of your loan.

7 min readUpdated April 2026

Fixed-Rate vs. Adjustable-Rate Mortgages

The first and most fundamental choice you'll make is whether to get a fixed-rate mortgage (FRM) or an adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM). Each has real tradeoffs that depend heavily on how long you plan to stay in the home and where interest rates are headed.

With a fixed-rate mortgage, your interest rate is locked in for the entire life of the loan. Your principal and interest payment never changes, making budgeting predictable and straightforward. This is the right choice for most people who plan to stay in their home long-term or who value payment stability above all else.

An adjustable-rate mortgage starts with a fixed rate for an initial period — typically 5, 7, or 10 years — and then adjusts annually based on a benchmark index (such as the Secured Overnight Financing Rate, or SOFR) plus a margin set by your lender. ARMs usually offer a lower initial rate than comparable fixed-rate loans, which can translate to meaningful savings in the early years. However, once the adjustment period begins, your rate — and payment — can rise significantly.

ARMs make the most sense if you're confident you'll sell or refinance before the fixed period ends, or if you believe rates will fall and you plan to refinance before they adjust upward. In a high-rate environment, an ARM's lower initial rate can provide real relief, but carry the risk carefully.

15-Year vs. 30-Year Loan Terms

After choosing your loan type, the next major decision is term length. The two most common options are 15-year and 30-year mortgages, and they represent fundamentally different financial strategies.

A 30-year mortgage spreads payments over a longer period, resulting in a lower monthly payment. This frees up cash flow each month for investing, emergencies, or other expenses. The downside is that you pay interest over twice as long, which means the total interest paid is dramatically higher.

A 15-year mortgage comes with a higher monthly payment but a significantly lower interest rate (typically 0.5–0.75% lower than a 30-year), and you pay off the loan in half the time. The total interest paid over the life of a 15-year loan can be less than half of what you'd pay on a 30-year.

Understanding Your Monthly Payment

Your mortgage payment is calculated using a standard amortization formula that distributes each payment between interest (front-loaded early in the loan) and principal (which grows over time).

M = P × [r(1 + r)n] / [(1 + r)n − 1]
M = monthly payment P = principal (loan amount) r = monthly interest rate (annual rate / 12) n = total number of payments (years × 12)

Keep in mind that your actual monthly payment typically includes more than just principal and interest. Lenders usually require you to escrow funds for property taxes and homeowners insurance, both of which are added to your monthly bill. These can add $300–$800 or more per month depending on your location and home value.

Down Payment, PMI, and Loan Types

Your down payment has a major impact on your loan costs, rate, and whether you'll need to pay Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI). Conventional loans require PMI when your down payment is less than 20% of the home's purchase price. PMI typically costs 0.5–1.5% of the loan amount annually and is added to your monthly payment.

On a $400,000 loan, PMI could add $167–$500 per month. The good news: once you reach 20% equity in the home — through payments and/or appreciation — you can request that PMI be removed. It's automatically cancelled when you reach 22% equity based on your original amortization schedule.

Different loan programs have different down payment requirements:

  • Conventional loans — as low as 3% down, but PMI applies below 20%
  • FHA loans — 3.5% down with a credit score of 580+; includes mortgage insurance premium (MIP) that lasts the life of the loan for most borrowers
  • VA loans — 0% down for eligible veterans and active-duty military; no PMI
  • USDA loans — 0% down for eligible rural properties; comes with a guarantee fee

A larger down payment reduces your loan balance, potentially gets you a better rate, and eliminates PMI sooner — but tying up too much cash in home equity can leave you financially vulnerable if unexpected expenses arise.

Points, Fees, and the True Cost of Your Loan

Mortgage lenders charge various fees at closing, and understanding them is essential to comparing loan offers accurately. The Annual Percentage Rate (APR) — not the interest rate alone — reflects the true cost of borrowing because it includes most fees spread over the loan term.

Discount points are upfront fees paid to lower your interest rate. One point equals 1% of the loan amount (so $4,000 on a $400,000 loan) and typically lowers your rate by 0.25%. Whether buying points makes sense depends on your break-even timeline — if you'll stay in the home long enough to recoup the upfront cost through lower monthly payments, points can be worthwhile.

Points Break-Even Example

You pay 1 point ($4,000) to reduce your rate from 6.75% to 6.50% on a $400,000 30-year loan.

Monthly savings at 6.50% vs. 6.75%: approximately $66/month.

Break-even: $4,000 ÷ $66 = ~61 months (5.1 years)
If you stay longer than 5 years, buying the point saves you money.

Other common closing costs include origination fees (0.5–1% of the loan), appraisal fees ($400–$700), title insurance, attorney fees, and prepaid items like the first year of homeowners insurance and property tax escrow. Total closing costs typically run 2–5% of the loan amount.

How to Compare Lenders and Loan Offers

Getting multiple loan offers is one of the most financially impactful steps you can take. Research consistently shows that borrowers who get at least three quotes save thousands of dollars compared to those who go with the first offer. Within a 45-day window, multiple mortgage inquiries count as a single hard pull on your credit, so shopping around won't hurt your score.

When comparing offers, don't look at interest rate alone. Use the Loan Estimate form — which lenders are required to provide within three business days of application — to compare APR, closing costs, loan terms, and whether the rate is locked. Also check whether the lender services their own loans or sells them, which affects who you'll deal with for the next 15–30 years.

📋 Checklist

Questions to Ask Every Lender

  • What is the APR (not just the interest rate) for this loan?
  • What are the total estimated closing costs, and which are negotiable?
  • How long will the quoted rate be locked in, and what does a lock extension cost?
  • Do you charge an origination fee, and can it be reduced?
  • Is there a prepayment penalty if I pay off the loan early?
  • Will you service this loan, or will it be sold to another lender?
  • What's the minimum down payment to avoid PMI with your conventional loan?
  • What credit score and debt-to-income ratio do you require for this rate?

Your debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is critical. Most lenders prefer a front-end DTI (housing costs / gross income) below 28% and a back-end DTI (all debt payments / gross income) below 43%. Knowing these numbers before you apply helps you understand how much home you can realistically afford — and prevents you from being approved for more than is financially wise.
