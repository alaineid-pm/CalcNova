Understanding Your Pregnancy Due Date: How It's Calculated

A pregnancy due date is not a delivery appointment — it's a midpoint of a statistical range. Understanding how doctors calculate it (and why) helps you interpret what "40 weeks" actually means for you and your baby.

6 min readUpdated April 2026

Naegele's Rule: The Formula Behind Every Due Date

The vast majority of due dates given by doctors and midwives worldwide are calculated using Naegele's Rule, a formula developed by German obstetrician Franz Karl Naegele in the early 19th century. Despite being nearly 200 years old, it remains the standard starting point — largely because it is simple, reproducible, and reasonably accurate for the average pregnancy.

The formula works from the first day of your Last Menstrual Period (LMP) and adds 280 days (40 weeks) to arrive at the estimated due date (EDD). A common shorthand: add 7 days to the LMP date, then subtract 3 months and add 1 year.

EDD = LMP + 280 days
EDD = Estimated Due Date LMP = First day of Last Menstrual Period 280 days = 40 weeks = approximately 9 months + 7 days

The shorthand version of Naegele's Rule: take the first day of your LMP, add 7 days, then add 9 months (or subtract 3 months and add 1 year). For example, if your LMP began on June 1, add 7 days (June 8), then add 9 months: due date is approximately March 8 of the following year.

Modern obstetric software and calculators perform this arithmetic automatically, but understanding the underlying formula helps you verify dates and understand why your provider may adjust the estimate based on ultrasound measurements.

Why LMP Instead of Conception Date?

The most common question about due date calculation: why does the count start from the last period rather than the day you conceived? After all, the embryo doesn't exist until conception — so the first two weeks of a "40-week pregnancy" are technically before pregnancy even begins.

The answer is practical, not biological. The exact date of conception is almost never known with certainty, even for people who track their cycles carefully. Ovulation typically occurs around day 14 of a 28-day cycle, but this varies considerably between individuals and from cycle to cycle. Sperm can survive in the reproductive tract for up to five days, meaning conception can occur several days after intercourse.

By contrast, the first day of the last menstrual period is a concrete, reliably recalled date. It provides a fixed, objective anchor for the calculation. Before routine ultrasound, LMP was the only practical starting point. The 40-week convention simply incorporates this two-week offset — so a pregnancy's gestational age at conception is approximately two weeks, and the 40-week count includes those first two "pre-conception" weeks as part of the standard timeline.

What "Full Term" Actually Means

For decades, any birth between 37 and 42 weeks was considered "full term." Medical science has since refined this classification significantly. Research showed that outcomes vary meaningfully even within this range, prompting the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) to introduce more precise terminology in 2013.

The window from 39 to 41 weeks represents the optimal range for delivery by most clinical measures. Babies born at 39–40 weeks have the lowest rates of respiratory complications, NICU admission, and neonatal death compared to those born earlier or later.

Trimester Breakdown: Key Milestones Week by Week

Pregnancy is conventionally divided into three trimesters, each roughly 13 weeks long. Each trimester is characterized by distinct developmental milestones for the fetus and different physical experiences for the pregnant person. Understanding the timeline helps contextualize prenatal appointments, screenings, and the changes happening throughout the 40 weeks.

Trimester Weeks Key Fetal Milestones Common Screenings
First Weeks 1–13 All major organs begin forming; heartbeat detectable by week 6; embryo becomes fetus at week 10 First prenatal visit, blood type, genetic carrier screening, nuchal translucency ultrasound (weeks 11–13)
Second Weeks 14–27 Sex organs distinguishable; fetal movement felt (quickening) around week 18–22; viability threshold ~24 weeks Anatomy ultrasound (week 20), glucose tolerance test (week 24–28), fetal movement monitoring
Third Weeks 28–40 Rapid weight gain; lungs mature; baby settles head-down; brain development accelerates Group B strep test (week 36), weekly appointments from week 36, non-stress tests if overdue

The second trimester is often called the "honeymoon trimester" — morning sickness typically subsides, energy returns, and the pregnancy becomes visible but the discomforts of late pregnancy haven't yet arrived. The third trimester brings rapid fetal growth, back pain, frequent urination, and Braxton Hicks contractions as the body prepares for labor.

Why Only About 5% of Babies Are Born on Their Due Date

The estimated due date is precisely that: an estimate. It represents the midpoint of a normal statistical distribution of delivery times, not a predicted appointment. In reality, spontaneous labor occurs across a range of gestational ages, with only about 4–5% of births happening exactly on the calculated due date.

The majority of births — roughly 80% — occur within two weeks of the due date (between 38 and 42 weeks). Several factors contribute to variability in delivery timing:

  • Cycle length: Naegele's Rule assumes a 28-day cycle with ovulation on day 14. Women with longer or shorter cycles may have a true conception date that differs by several days from the LMP-based assumption.
  • Biological variability: Even with a precisely known conception date, the normal range for human gestation spans several weeks.
  • First vs. subsequent pregnancies: First-time mothers tend to deliver slightly later on average than those who have given birth before.
  • Ultrasound dating: Early ultrasound (before 14 weeks) can revise the due date by measuring the embryo's size, which is often more accurate than LMP alone for women with irregular cycles.

Providers increasingly use ultrasound measurements alongside LMP to arrive at an estimated due date. When the two methods differ by more than a week or two, most providers adjust the due date based on the ultrasound, which is a more direct measurement of gestational age than calendar math.

Planned Deliveries: Induction and C-Section Timing

Not all births begin with spontaneous labor. A significant proportion of deliveries in the U.S. — roughly 26–30% — are cesarean sections, and a large share of vaginal births involve medical induction. Understanding the timing considerations around planned deliveries is increasingly relevant for expectant parents.

The landmark ARRIVE Trial (2018) found that elective induction at 39 weeks did not increase cesarean rates and was associated with slightly lower C-section rates compared to waiting for spontaneous labor. This shifted many providers toward offering induction at 39 weeks rather than waiting until 41 or 42 weeks.

Planned C-sections for non-emergency reasons (such as a previous uterine surgery or planned repeat cesarean) are typically scheduled at 39 weeks, balancing fetal maturity against the risks of spontaneous labor beginning before the planned date. Emergency or urgent C-sections follow different timing governed by the specific clinical situation.

⚠️ Important Note

Due Dates Are Starting Points, Not Deadlines

A due date should be understood as the center of a range — roughly 38 to 42 weeks — not as a target or a deadline. Becoming anxious as the due date approaches and passes is extremely common, but most providers will not intervene before 41 weeks in a low-risk pregnancy unless there are specific clinical concerns. Every pregnancy and provider relationship is different; discuss your specific timeline and preferences with your obstetric care team.
